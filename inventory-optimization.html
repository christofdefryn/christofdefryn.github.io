Inventory Optimization in Omnichannel Retail

Adaptive inventory policies for retailers that must coordinate stock across physical stores and online channels while dynamically learning demand patterns — combining inventory theory with Bayesian statistics.

Inventory Theory Demand Learning Omnichannel Bayesian Methods Retail Operations

Overview

The rise of omnichannel retail has fundamentally changed the inventory management problem. Customers expect seamless access to products regardless of channel — they may browse online, purchase in-store, return via mail, or pick up curbside. This means retailers must simultaneously manage inventory pools across multiple locations while demand patterns are highly heterogeneous, non-stationary, and only partially observable through each channel.

This project develops the theoretical foundations and practical algorithms for inventory optimization in omnichannel environments. We study how retailers should set stocking levels and replenishment triggers when they must balance channel-specific demand uncertainty, inventory pooling opportunities, and fulfillment costs, all while learning about an evolving customer demand distribution.

Research Questions

  • What is the optimal structure of inventory replenishment policies when demand is uncertain and learning occurs dynamically?
  • How much value does inventory pooling across channels create, and under what conditions?
  • How should a retailer balance the exploration–exploitation tradeoff in demand learning?
  • Can we develop computationally tractable policies with provable performance guarantees?

Key Findings

Our main theoretical result shows that the optimal policy under Bayesian demand updating retains a base-stock structure, generalizing classical newsvendor-type results to the dynamic learning setting. The optimal base-stock level is shown to decrease over time as uncertainty resolves, with the rate of decrease depending on the prior variance and the channel correlation structure.

On the pooling side, we quantify the value of flexible fulfillment (i.e., fulfilling online orders from physical stores and vice versa) and characterize the conditions — primarily related to the coefficient of variation of channel demands — under which pooling is most beneficial. Numerical experiments with real retail data confirm the theoretical predictions.
