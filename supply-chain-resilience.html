Supply Chain Resilience under Climate and Disruption Risk

Designing supply networks that stay cost-effective under demand uncertainty, supplier disruptions, and climate-driven shocks — using two-stage stochastic programming and robust optimization.

Stochastic Programming Network Design Resilience Climate Risk Supply Chain

Overview

Modern supply chains face an increasingly volatile landscape: demand shocks, geopolitical disruptions, supplier failures, and more recently, the systemic effects of climate change on infrastructure, agriculture, and transportation. This project develops mathematical optimization models that allow firms and policymakers to design supply networks that are both efficient under normal conditions and resilient when disruptions occur.

The core methodological contribution is a family of two-stage stochastic programming models in which first-stage decisions (facility locations, capacity investments, long-term contracts) are made before uncertainty is resolved, and second-stage decisions (flow allocation, emergency sourcing) adapt to realized scenarios. We develop exact decomposition algorithms (Benders, L-shaped) and heuristics for large-scale instances.

Research Questions

  • How should a firm balance cost efficiency and resilience when designing a multi-echelon supply network?
  • What is the economic value of resilience investments under different disruption probability profiles?
  • How do climate change trajectories affect optimal network topology over long planning horizons?
  • Can we develop tractable models that scale to realistic industry instances?

Key Findings

Our results show that moderate investments in redundancy — dual sourcing and regional safety stocks — yield disproportionate resilience improvements at relatively low cost under a broad range of disruption scenarios. Climate-adjusted models suggest that network designs optimized under historical disruption data become significantly suboptimal when projected climate trajectories are incorporated.

The decomposition algorithms developed in this project solve instances with up to 500 nodes and 1,000 scenarios within practical runtimes, enabling their use in commercial decision-support settings.
